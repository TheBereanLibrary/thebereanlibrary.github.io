Confession

Doctrinal Statement

The Berean Library is a non-denominational, evangelical project committed to historic Christian orthodoxy. We gladly welcome rigorous scholarship and charitable dialogue while maintaining fidelity to Scripture as the final authority in all matters of faith and practice (Acts 17:11; 2 Tim 3:16–17).

1. Scripture

Definition. The sixty-six books of the Old and New Testaments are the Word of God, inspired by the Holy Spirit, inerrant in all they affirm, and wholly sufficient as the final authority for faith and practice.

  • Key texts. 2 Tim 3:16–17; 2 Pet 1:20–21; John 10:35; Ps 19; Matt 5:17–18.
  • Historical notes. Recognition of the biblical canon in the early church (Athanasius, AD 367; regional councils of Hippo and Carthage).
  • Implications. Scripture interprets Scripture; translations serve the text; the church receives rather than creates the canon.
  • Errors rejected. Denials of inspiration, infallibility, or sufficiency; extra-biblical authorities that overturn Scripture.

2. The Triune God

Definition. There is one God, eternally existing in three coequal, coeternal Persons—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit—of one essence and glory.

  • Key texts. Deut 6:4; Matt 28:19; John 1:1–3; 10:30; Acts 5:3–4; 2 Cor 13:14.
  • Historical notes. Nicene faith (325/381); Chalcedonian Christology (451) safeguards Trinitarian confession.
  • Errors rejected. Arianism, Modalism, Subordinationism, Tritheism.

3. The Person and Work of Christ

Person. The eternal Son became truly man, conceived by the Holy Spirit, born of the virgin Mary; one Person in two natures without confusion, change, division, or separation.

Work. In obedience to the Father He lived sinlessly, died a substitutionary death, rose bodily, ascended, and will return in glory. His cross secures redemption, propitiation, reconciliation, and victory over the powers.

  • Key texts. John 1:14; Phil 2:5–11; Heb 2:14–17; 1 Cor 15:1–8; Rom 3:21–26; 2 Cor 5:21; Col 2:13–15.
  • Errors rejected. Docetism, Adoptionism, Nestorianism, Eutychianism; denials of the bodily resurrection.

4. The Holy Spirit

Definition. The Holy Spirit is the Lord and giver of life, proceeding from the Father and the Son, who regenerates, indwells, sanctifies, equips, and unites believers to Christ and to one another.

  • Key texts. John 14–16; Rom 8; 1 Cor 12–14; Gal 5:16–26; Eph 1:13–14.
  • Implications. The Spirit never contradicts Scripture; He grants diverse gifts for the common good and mission.
  • Errors rejected. Treating the Spirit as impersonal force; ongoing revelation that overturns Scripture.

5. Humanity, Sin, and the Image of God

Humanity. Created male and female in God’s image, endowed with dignity and vocation to reflect God and steward creation.

Sin. Through Adam’s historical fall humanity became guilty and corrupted; death and disorder spread to all. The image is marred but not erased; renewal comes in Christ.

  • Key texts. Gen 1:26–28; 2:7; 3; Ps 8; Rom 1:18–32; 3:9–23; 5:12–21; 1 Cor 15:21–22; Jas 3:9.
  • Errors rejected. Denial of historical Adam; reduction of sin to mere social structures; devaluation of embodied personhood.

6. Salvation

Definition. Salvation is by grace alone through faith alone in Christ alone to the glory of God alone; it entails election, effectual calling, regeneration, conversion, justification, adoption, sanctification, perseverance, and final glorification.

  • Key texts. John 3; 6; Acts 16:31; Rom 3–8; Eph 1–2; Titus 3:3–7; 1 Pet 1.
  • Justification. God declares sinners righteous on the basis of Christ’s righteousness imputed and received by faith apart from works (Rom 3:21–28; 4:1–8; 5:1).
  • Union with Christ. All saving benefits flow from union with the risen Lord (Eph 1; Rom 6).
  • Errors rejected. Any gospel that adds human merit as the ground of acceptance; universalism; denial of new birth.

7. The Church

Definition. The one, holy, catholic, and apostolic church is the people of God in all ages, gathered in local congregations marked by the Word rightly preached, the ordinances rightly administered, and discipline lovingly practiced.

  • Key texts. Matt 16:18–19; 18:15–20; Acts 2:42–47; Eph 2:19–22; 4:1–16; 1 Tim 3; Titus 1.
  • Mission. The Great Commission to make disciples of all nations through proclamation, baptism, and teaching (Matt 28:18–20).
  • Unity and diversity. We affirm generous cooperation with all who hold the biblical gospel while maintaining convictional integrity on secondary matters.

8. Baptism & the Lord’s Supper

Baptism. An ordinance commanded by Christ for believers as a sign of union with Him in His death and resurrection.

The Supper. A participation in Christ by the Spirit, instituted in remembrance of Him; a means of grace for the faithful and to be received with self-examination.

  • Key texts. Matt 28:19; Acts 2:38–41; Rom 6:3–5; 1 Cor 10:16–17; 11:23–32.
  • Charity. We practice charity toward differing evangelical traditions (timing/mode of baptism; presence in the Supper) while holding Scripture supreme.

9. Christian Life & Ethics

Sanctification. Progressive growth in holiness by the Spirit through the Word, prayer, ordinances, and fellowship.

Vocation & Justice. Christians pursue love of neighbor, honesty, sexual purity, generosity, and care for the vulnerable; they reject partiality and violence, seeking the common good under Christ’s lordship.

  • Key texts. Matt 5–7; John 13:34–35; Rom 12; Gal 5; Eph 4–6; Jas 1–2.

10. Last Things

We confess the blessed hope of Christ’s visible, bodily return; the resurrection of the dead; the final judgment; the new heavens and new earth where righteousness dwells. We welcome responsible diversity on the timing of millennial matters within historic orthodoxy.

  • Key texts. Matt 24–25; John 5:28–29; Acts 1:11; 1 Cor 15; 1 Thess 4:13–18; 2 Pet 3; Rev 20–22.

Notes & Key Texts

Scripture: Ps 19; Deut 6:4; Isa 40:8; Matt 5:17–18; 16:18–19; 28:18–20; John 1; 3; 14–16; Acts 2; Rom 3–8; 1 Cor 10–15; 2 Cor 5; Gal 5; Eph 1–4; Phil 2; Col 1–2; 1 Thess 4; 1–2 Tim; Titus 3; Heb 1–2; 9–10; 1 Pet 1; 2 Pet 1; 3; Rev 21–22.

Historical markers: Nicene Creed (325/381); Chalcedon (451); Athanasius Festal Letter 39 (AD 367); regional councils at Hippo (393) and Carthage (397).
