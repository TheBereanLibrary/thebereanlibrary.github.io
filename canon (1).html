Foundations

Canon Formation

How the church received and recognized the sixty-six books of Holy Scripture, hearing in them the voice of the Shepherd (John 10:27).

Contents 1. Old Testament: Shape and Reception 2. New Testament: Apostolic Writings and Reception 3. Descriptive Criteria 4. Self-Authentication 5. Preservation, Text, and Translation 6. Patristic and Historical Witnesses Annotated Bibliography Notes

1. Old Testament: Shape and Reception

Threefold structure: Law, Prophets, Writings (Luke 24:44). Custody near the ark (Deut 31:24–26), covenant reading (Deut 31:10–13; Neh 8), and royal reform by the book (2 Kgs 22–23) display Scripture’s authority in Israel’s life.

  • Second Temple attestations: Prologue to Sirach (“Law, Prophets, and the others”); Josephus’ twenty-two books (Against Apion 1.8) aligning by count with the Protestant thirty-nine.
  • The Writings’ fluid order in antiquity does not imply open canon; rather, a settled body with local ordering.

2. New Testament: Apostolic Writings and Reception

The NT arises from Christ’s commission and the Spirit’s guidance (John 14:26; 16:13). Churches circulate and publicly read the apostolic writings (Col 4:16; 1 Thess 5:27), and Peter already classifies Paul’s letters with “the other Scriptures” (2 Pet 3:15–16).

  • Muratorian Fragment (late 2nd c.); Irenaeus on the four Gospels; Athanasius (AD 367) enumerates twenty-seven; regional councils (Hippo 393; Carthage 397) echo reception.
  • Eusebius’ categories (EH 3.25) describe acknowledged, disputed, and spurious works without granting authority to the latter.

3. Descriptive Criteria

  1. Apostolicity (or sanctioned associates).
  2. Orthodoxy (rule of faith; Gal 1:8–9).
  3. Catholicity (widespread, continuous use).
  4. Divine qualities (Spirit’s self-authenticating testimony; 1 Cor 2:12–14).

4. Self-Authentication

God-breathed (2 Tim 3:16–17), Spirit-carried authors (2 Pet 1:20–21), unbreakable word (John 10:35). The church recognizes, not creates, the canon (Jas 1:18; 1 Pet 1:23).

5. Preservation, Text, and Translation

  • Providential preservation (Isa 40:8; Matt 5:18). Textual criticism refines our access to the earliest text; no cardinal doctrine hangs on a single disputed reading.
  • Translation philosophy: formal vs functional; a family of faithful translations serves the church (Neh 8:8).

6. Patristic & Historical Witnesses

Short Quotations

“The Scriptures are indeed perfect, since they were spoken by the Word of God.” — Irenaeus, Against Heresies 2.28 (short excerpt)
“Let the divinely inspired Scripture be our umpire.” — Athanasius, De Decretis 31 (short excerpt)

Conciliar Touchpoints

  • Athanasius, Festal Letter 39 (AD 367) lists the twenty-seven NT books.
  • Regional councils at Hippo (393) and Carthage (397) recognize the same corpus.

Annotated Bibliography

  • F. F. Bruce, The Canon of Scripture. Classic historical survey, accessible yet rigorous.
  • Lee Martin McDonald, The Biblical Canon. Detailed treatment of OT/NT development.
  • Michael J. Kruger, Canon Revisited. Theological account of self-authentication.
