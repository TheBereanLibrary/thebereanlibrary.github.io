Redemption

The Atonement: Biblical Witness and Theological Models

Christ’s cross and resurrection accomplish substitution, victory, reconciliation, ransom, and moral transformation.

Contents 1. Sacrifice and Substitution 2. Penal Substitution 3. Christus Victor 4. Moral Influence 5. Governmental Emphasis 6. Patristic Witnesses Annotated Bibliography Notes

1. Sacrifice and Substitution

Passover (Exod 12), Day of Atonement (Lev 16), and the Suffering Servant (Isa 53) converge on Christ, the Lamb of God (John 1:29; 1 Cor 5:7).

2. Penal Substitution

God presents Christ as propitiation to demonstrate His righteousness and justify the believer (Rom 3:21–26). “He gave his life as a ransom for many” (Mark 10:45); “the righteous for the unrighteous” (1 Pet 3:18).

3. Christus Victor

Christ disarms rulers and authorities (Col 2:13–15), destroys the devil’s works (1 John 3:8), and frees us from fear of death (Heb 2:14–15).

4. Moral Influence

The cross reveals love (Rom 5:8) and patterns discipleship (1 Pet 2:21) as an effect, not the sole mechanism.

5. Governmental Emphasis

Highlights how the cross upholds moral order while granting mercy (cf. Rom 3:25–26).

6. Patristic Witnesses

“He became what we are that He might make us what He is.” — Athanasius, On the Incarnation 54 (short excerpt)
“By his blood we have been redeemed.” — Ignatius of Antioch, To the Ephesians 1 (short excerpt)

Annotated Bibliography

  • Leon Morris, The Apostolic Preaching of the Cross. Word studies on redemption, propitiation, reconciliation.
  • John Stott, The Cross of Christ. Pastoral and theological classic.
  • Thomas Schreiner, The King in His Beauty. Canonical synthesis emphasizing substitution and kingdom.

Notes

Primary: Leviticus 16; Exodus 12; Isaiah 52:13–53:12; Mark 10:45; Romans 3:21–26; 5; 8; 2 Corinthians 5:21; Colossians 2:13–15; Hebrews 2:14–15; 9–10; 1 Peter 2:24; 3:18.
