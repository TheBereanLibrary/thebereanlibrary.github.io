Origins

Creation: Exegesis, Theology, and Christian Confession

Within the catholic confession of God as Creator, faithful interpreters have offered differing accounts of Genesis 1–2 while preserving core dogma.

Contents 1. Exegesis of Genesis 1–2 2. Major Orthodox Views 3. Image of God 4. Fall and Its Effects 5. Christ and Creation 6. Patristic Witnesses Annotated Bibliography Notes

1. Exegesis of Genesis 1–2

Temple motifs, forming/filling structure, Sabbath telos (Gen 2:2–3). Adam as priest-king (Gen 2:15); dominion and stewardship (Gen 1:26–28).

2. Major Orthodox Views

  • Young Earth Creation: six 24-hour days; strengths—plain reading, Adam–Christ typology (Rom 5; 1 Cor 15).
  • Old Earth / Analogical Day / Framework: days as God’s workdays or literary structure; seeks harmony with general revelation (Ps 19:1–4); retains historical Adam.

3. Image of God

Representation, relationality, righteousness (Gen 1:26–28; 2:7; Eph 4:24; Col 3:10). Image marred, not erased (Gen 9:6; Jas 3:9); renewed in Christ (Col 1:15; 2 Cor 4:4).

4. Fall and Its Effects

Historical disobedience (Gen 3) leading to death (Rom 5:12) and cosmic groaning (Rom 8:20–22), yet hope through the promised seed (Gen 3:15).

5. Christ and Creation

Through Him and for Him (Col 1:16); He upholds all things (Heb 1:3); resurrection inaugurates new creation (2 Cor 5:17).

6. Patristic Witnesses

“The Maker of all things is one God.” — Theophilus of Antioch, To Autolycus 2.4 (short excerpt)
“He made all things out of nothing through His Word.” — Irenaeus, Against Heresies 2.10 (short excerpt)

Annotated Bibliography

  • John Walton, The Lost World of Genesis One. Ancient Near Eastern background with a cosmic-temple reading.
  • C. John Collins, Genesis 1–4. Linguistic and literary analysis; analogical day approach.
  • Henri Blocher, In the Beginning. Classic evangelical synthesis.

Notes

Primary: Genesis 1–3; Psalm 8; Exodus 20:11; Romans 5; 8; 1 Corinthians 15; John 1; Colossians 1; Hebrews 1; Revelation 21–22.
