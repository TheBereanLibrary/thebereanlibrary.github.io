Q&A Repository on Christian Theology

1) What is the Trinity, and how is it grounded in Scripture?

Answer: The one true God eternally exists in three distinct Persons—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit—each fully and equally God, not three gods nor mere modes, but one divine essence in three Persons. The Father begets the Son; the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son. The works of God are undivided, fittingly appropriated to each Person.

Key texts: Deut 6:4; Matt 28:19; John 1:1–18; 14–16; 2 Cor 13:14.

Guardrails: Reject tritheism (three gods) and modalism (one person with three masks).

Why it matters: Salvation is from the Father, through the Son, by the Spirit; Christian worship is inherently Trinitarian.

2) What are God’s attributes (incommunicable & communicable)?

Answer: Incommunicable attributes (aseity, simplicity, immutability, infinity, omnipresence, omniscience, omnipotence) mark God’s uniqueness; communicable attributes (holiness, justice, goodness, love, mercy, truth, wisdom) are reflected in creatures analogically. God is simple (not composed of parts); His attributes are identical with His being.

Key texts: Exod 3:14; Ps 90:2; Mal 3:6; Ps 139; 1 John 4:8; Exod 34:6–7; Rom 11:33–36.

Guardrails: Avoid treating attributes as parts, or implying God changes in His being/purposes.

Why it matters: Confidence in prayer and worship rests on who God is—unchanging, all-wise, perfectly good.

3) What is divine providence, and how does it relate to human freedom?

Answer: Providence is God’s preservation, concurrence, and government of all things. He ordains whatsoever comes to pass without being the author of sin or overriding real creaturely agency.

Key texts: Ps 104; Prov 16:9; Matt 10:29–31; Acts 17:24–28; Gen 50:20; Eph 1:11.

Guardrails: Reject fatalism (denies responsibility) and deism (denies God’s active care). Historic compatibilism affirms both sovereignty and responsibility (Acts 2:23).

Why it matters: Grounds assurance in suffering and fuels active, prayerful obedience.

4) What does it mean that humans are made in the image of God (imago Dei)?

Answer: Humanity—male and female—bears God’s image with dignity, relational capacity, moral agency, and a vocation to represent God by filling, ruling, and cultivating creation. The image is marred by sin yet renewed in Christ.

Key texts: Gen 1:26–28; 2:15; 9:6; Ps 8; Eph 4:24; Col 3:10; Jas 3:9.

Guardrails: Don’t reduce the image to reason alone or dominion alone; it is holistic. The fall distorts, not erases.

Why it matters: Grounds human dignity, clarifies vocation, and frames justice and mercy.

5) What is the fall and original sin?

Answer: In Adam, humanity fell from original righteousness; we inherit guilt and a corrupt nature inclined to evil. Apart from grace we are spiritually dead and unable to turn to God.

Key texts: Gen 3; Ps 51:5; Rom 3:9–20; 5:12–21; Eph 2:1–3.

Guardrails: Reject Pelagianism (humans born morally neutral). Responsibility remains; grace truly renews.

Why it matters: A right diagnosis makes the **grace-alone** gospel necessary and glorious.

6) Who is Jesus Christ? (the person of Christ)

Answer: The eternal Son assumed a complete human nature in the incarnation, remaining one person in two natures—divine and human—without confusion, change, division, or separation. True God and true man, He is the sole mediator.

Key texts: John 1:1–14; Phil 2:5–11; Col 1:15–20; Heb 1–2; 1 Tim 2:5.

Guardrails: Reject Arianism, Apollinarianism, Nestorianism, and Eutychianism.

Why it matters: Only God-with-us can save; only a true man can represent and redeem us.

7) What did Christ accomplish in His death and resurrection? (atonement)

Answer: The cross and resurrection secure penal substitution (bearing our guilt and curse to satisfy divine justice), victory over sin/death/Satan, ransom/redemption, reconciliation, and exemplar love—Scripture presents these facets with penal substitution at the doctrinal core of justification.

Key texts: Isa 52:13–53:12; Mark 10:45; Rom 3:21–26; 5:6–11; 8:1–4; 2 Cor 5:21; Col 2:13–15; 1 Pet 2:24; 3:18.

Guardrails: More than moral influence; and never a cold transaction—salvation is the work of the Triune God.

Why it matters: Assurance rests on Christ’s objective, finished work.

8) Who is the Holy Spirit and what is His work?

Answer: The Spirit is the third Person of the Trinity, fully God, proceeding from the Father and the Son. He regenerates, indwells, sanctifies, assures, illumines Scripture, unites us to Christ, and distributes gifts for the Church’s edification and mission.

Key texts: John 3:5–8; 14–16; Rom 8; 1 Cor 2:10–14; 12–14; Gal 5:16–26; Eph 1:13–14; Titus 3:5–6.

Guardrails: He is a Person, not an impersonal force; illumination is not new canonical revelation; gifts must edify in order and love (1 Cor 14).

Why it matters: Without the Spirit there is no new birth, holiness, unity, or power for mission.

9) What is Holy Scripture? (revelation, inspiration, canon, sufficiency)

Answer: God’s special revelation is inscripturated in the 66-book canon of the Old and New Testaments. Scripture is God-breathed, true in all it affirms, and sufficient for faith and practice. The Church recognized (did not create) the canon as apostolic/prophetic writings bearing divine qualities and corporate reception.

Key texts: 2 Tim 3:16–17; 2 Pet 1:19–21; Ps 19; John 10:35; Luke 24:27,44–47; Heb 1:1–2.

Guardrails: Scripture is the norming norm; tradition is a witness, not a rival. Canon is complete; the Spirit illumines what He has breathed out.

Why it matters: Discipleship rests on a trustworthy Word; sufficiency guards against adding or subtracting from God’s speech.

10) How does God save sinners? (union with Christ & the order of salvation)

Answer: United to Christ by the Spirit, sinners are effectually called, regenerated, granted repentance and faith, justified (imputed righteousness), adopted, sanctified (definitive & progressive), preserved, and glorified. Union with Christ is the organizing center: in Him we die to sin and rise to new life.

Key texts: Rom 8:28–30; John 6:37–40; 15:1–11; Eph 1:3–14; 2:1–10; Gal 2:20; Titus 3:3–7; Phil 1:6.

Guardrails: Justification by faith alone; sanctification is distinct but inseparable; God preserves His people while real warnings summon endurance.

Why it matters: Protects grace, fuels holiness, and anchors assurance in Christ’s finished work and the Spirit’s ongoing work.

Apologetics — Giving a Reason for the Hope Within

Defending and commending the faith with clarity, gentleness, and reverence (1 Pet 3:15).

1) How can we know God exists?

Answer: God’s existence is revealed in creation (general revelation), conscience, and supremely in Christ and Scripture (special revelation). Classical theistic arguments—cosmological (contingency), teleological (design), moral (objective moral values), ontological (maximally great being)—are supportive but God is ultimately known through His self-disclosure by the Spirit.

Key texts: Ps 19:1–6; Rom 1:18–23; Acts 14:15–17; 17:22–31; Heb 11:6.

Guardrails: Arguments do not replace revelation; faith is not irrational but Spirit-enabled trust in the God who has made Himself known.

2) If God is good, why is there evil and suffering?

Answer: God is perfectly good, yet permits evil for wise and holy purposes—displaying His justice, mercy, and glory in ways otherwise unseen. Moral evil stems from creaturely rebellion; natural evil from the curse following the fall. In Christ, God enters suffering, bears it, and will end it in the new creation.

Key texts: Gen 50:20; Job 1–2; Isa 53; Acts 2:23; Rom 8:18–39; Rev 21:1–5.

Guardrails: Avoid portraying God as the author of sin; affirm both sovereignty and creaturely agency. The cross is the lens for God’s goodness in suffering.

3) Is the Bible historically reliable?

Answer: Yes. Manuscript evidence is abundant and early; archaeological findings often confirm biblical details; fulfilled prophecy, literary coherence, and eyewitness testimony (esp. of the resurrection) provide cumulative credibility. The Spirit’s witness brings conviction that it is God’s Word.

Key texts: Luke 1:1–4; 1 Cor 15:3–8; 2 Pet 1:16–21; Ps 119:160.

Guardrails: Reliability does not mean every question is answered; inerrancy means Scripture affirms only truth, rightly interpreted.

4) How can miracles be possible in a scientific age?

Answer: If God created and sustains the universe, miracles are not violations of natural law but acts of the Creator, who is free to act beyond ordinary providence. Science describes regularities; it cannot preclude God’s special acts.

Key texts: Gen 1:1; Ps 135:6; Matt 19:26; John 20:30–31; Acts 2:22–24.

Guardrails: Don’t treat miracles as magic; they are purposeful signs pointing to God’s redemptive work.

5) What evidence is there for Jesus’ resurrection?

Answer: The resurrection is supported by multiple early independent testimonies, the empty tomb, post-mortem appearances to individuals and groups, the transformation of disciples, the rise of the Church, and the shift in worship to the first day. The best explanation is that Jesus truly rose bodily.

Key texts: 1 Cor 15:3–8; Matt 28; Luke 24; Acts 2:22–36.

Guardrails: Not merely a “spiritual” resurrection; Scripture presents it as bodily and historical.

6) How do we know Christianity is the only true way?

Answer: Jesus claimed exclusive authority (John 14:6), fulfilled unique messianic prophecy, performed public miracles, rose from the dead, and offers the only sufficient atonement. Other religions may contain moral truths but cannot reconcile sinners to God apart from Christ.

Key texts: Isa 45:21–22; John 14:6; Acts 4:12; 1 Tim 2:5–6.

Guardrails: Hold truth with humility and love; exclusivity is about the sufficiency of Christ, not cultural superiority.

7) How can faith and reason work together?

Answer: Faith is not irrational but trust in God based on who He is and what He has done; reason helps us understand, explain, and defend the faith. Faith seeks understanding (fides quaerens intellectum) and rests on the Spirit’s witness through God’s Word.

Key texts: Isa 1:18; Acts 17:2–4; 1 Pet 3:15; Jude 3.

Guardrails: Avoid fideism (faith with no reason) and rationalism (reason as ultimate authority).

8) What about those who have never heard the gospel?

Answer: All people have general revelation and are accountable for suppressing the truth; salvation comes only through Christ. God is just and merciful, and has ordained the preaching of the gospel to all nations. We entrust such cases to His justice and urgency of mission.

Key texts: Ps 19; Rom 1:18–23; 10:9–17; Acts 4:12.

Guardrails: Avoid universalism; uphold God’s justice and mercy; let this fuel evangelism, not apathy.

9) Can we trust the transmission of the Bible over time?

Answer: Yes. Textual criticism shows the New Testament is the best-preserved work of antiquity, with thousands of manuscripts across centuries allowing reconstruction with >99% accuracy; no essential doctrine depends on a disputed text.

Key texts: Isa 40:8; Matt 5:18; 24:35; 2 Tim 3:16.

Guardrails: Recognize minor variations exist; accuracy is about faithful preservation, not identical copies.

10) How should Christians engage in apologetics?

Answer: With gentleness and respect, rooted in Scripture, dependent on the Spirit, and aimed at persuasion and proclamation—not winning arguments. Apologetics serves the gospel, not pride.

Key texts: 1 Pet 3:15; Col 4:5–6; 2 Tim 2:24–26; Acts 17:16–34.

Guardrails: Avoid quarrelsomeness; the goal is to remove obstacles and present Christ clearly.

Church History & Practice — Timeline & Themes

A historical sweep with pastoral application, grounded in Scripture and classic orthodoxy.

1) Why was the Council of Nicaea (AD 325) historically necessary?

Answer: The Arian controversy denied the full deity of the Son. Nicaea convened to safeguard apostolic faith by confessing the Son as homoousios (“of the same essence”) with the Father. This preserved the biblical gospel: only if Christ is truly God can He truly save.

Key texts: John 1:1–3; Col 1:15–20; Heb 1:1–3; John 20:28; Matt 28:19.

Historical notes: ~318 bishops; creed drafted against Arianism; later clarified at Constantinople (381).

Why it matters: Trinitarian worship and salvation rest on Christ’s true deity.

2) What impact did the Reformation (16th c.) have on doctrine and worship?

Answer: It re-centered the Church on Scripture alone and justification by faith alone, reformed worship (vernacular Scripture, preaching, congregational singing), and recovered the primacy of grace.

Key texts: Rom 1:16–17; 3:21–28; Eph 2:8–9; Acts 17:11.

Historical notes: Luther, Calvin, Zwingli; confessions (Augsburg, Westminster, Belgic, Heidelberg).

Why it matters: Guards the gospel’s freeness and the church’s submission to the Word.

3) How did the Great Awakenings shape evangelical identity and missions?

Answer: Revivals emphasized new birth, earnest preaching, holiness, and global evangelization, fueling missionary societies and social reform, while insisting on experiential faith anchored in truth.

Key texts: Acts 2:37–41; John 3:3–8; Matt 28:18–20; 2 Tim 4:2.

Historical notes: Edwards, Whitefield; later Finney; rise of Bible/mission societies.

Why it matters: Evangelical zeal weds conviction with compassion and mission.

4) Why is the Lord’s Supper central, and how has its meaning varied?

Answer: Instituted by Christ, the Supper proclaims His death, nourishes faith, and expresses church unity. Views range from transubstantiation to spiritual presence to memorial; orthodox practice keeps Word, faith, self-examination, and unity at the center.

Key texts: Matt 26:26–29; 1 Cor 10:16–17; 11:23–29; Luke 24:30–35.

Why it matters: Shapes our communion with Christ and one another; guards against ritualism and irreverence.

5) What is the biblical pattern for church leadership (elders & deacons)?

Answer: The New Testament portrays a plurality of qualified elders (overseers/pastors) who teach and shepherd, and deacons who serve practical needs. Leadership is character-first and Word-centered.

Key texts: Acts 14:23; 20:28; 1 Tim 3:1–13; Titus 1:5–9; 1 Pet 5:1–4; Phil 1:1.

Why it matters: Healthy polity protects doctrine, care, and mission.

6) How have preaching methods developed, and why is expositional preaching vital?

Answer: From apostolic proclamation to patristic homilies to Reformation exposition, faithful preaching opens the text and applies it to God’s people. Exposition honors the authority and intent of Scripture.

Key texts: Neh 8:8; Luke 24:27; Acts 2; 20:27; 2 Tim 4:1–2.

Why it matters: Guards against hobbyhorses and ensures God’s Word sets the agenda.

7) How has the Church thought about social justice and mercy?

Answer: From early diaconal care to abolitionist movements and hospital/education initiatives, Christians have pursued justice and mercy as fruit of the gospel, not a substitute for it; ethical action flows from regeneration and discipleship.

Key texts: Mic 6:8; Isa 1:17; Matt 25:35–40; Luke 10:25–37; Jas 1:27; 2:14–17.

Why it matters: Keeps orthodoxy and orthopraxy together; resists both quietism and a gospel-of-works.

8) What did monastic and renewal movements contribute to Christian formation?

Answer: Monasticism preserved texts, modeled disciplined prayer, service, and learning; later renewal movements (e.g., mendicant orders, Pietism) stressed holiness and devotion. Their strengths inspire disciplined discipleship; their excesses warn against legalism or withdrawal.

Key texts: Matt 6:5–18; 1 Cor 7:5; 1 Tim 4:7–8; Heb 10:24–25.

Why it matters: Encourages ordered piety shaped by Word and community.

9) How has Christian song shaped doctrine (hymns to modern worship)?

Answer: The Church sings truth into memory; from psalms and early hymns to Reformation chorales to contemporary songs, robust lyrics catechize the heart. Musical forms vary; theological fidelity and congregational participation remain non-negotiables.

Key texts: Ps 96; 98; Col 3:16; Eph 5:19; Rev 5:9–13.

Why it matters: We become what we sing; prioritize biblically rich, Christ-exalting congregational song.

10) What does “unity in essentials, liberty in non-essentials, charity in all things” mean today?

Answer: The Church seeks unity in core gospel truths (e.g., Trinity, incarnation, atonement, justification), liberty on secondary matters (forms of polity, some end-times details), and love in every debate. Biblical unity never compromises truth; charity shapes tone and method.

Key texts: John 17:20–23; Eph 4:1–6, 11–16; Rom 14; 15:5–7; Jude 3.

Why it matters: Fosters cooperation for mission while guarding confessional integrity.

Christian Living & Discipleship — Doctrine in Daily Life

A biblically grounded vision of the Spirit-filled life, sanctification, and faithful witness.

1) What does it mean to “deny yourself, take up your cross, and follow” Christ?

Answer: It means renouncing self-rule, embracing God’s will, enduring hardship for Christ’s sake, and living for His glory above all else. It’s the essence of discipleship: death to self, life in Christ.

Key texts: Matt 16:24–26; Luke 9:23; Gal 2:20; Phil 3:7–11.

Why it matters: Christianity without the cross is counterfeit; true discipleship involves cost and joy.

2) How does the Holy Spirit empower sanctification?

Answer: The Spirit indwells believers, applying Christ’s work, producing holiness, illuminating Scripture, granting victory over sin, and conforming us to Christ’s image.

Key texts: Rom 8:1–14; Gal 5:16–25; 2 Cor 3:18; Phil 2:12–13.

Why it matters: Avoids both self-reliance and passivity; sanctification is Spirit-empowered yet believer-engaged.

3) What role does Scripture play in spiritual growth?

Answer: Scripture is God’s living Word, sufficient for teaching, rebuke, correction, and training in righteousness. It feeds the soul, renews the mind, and equips for every good work.

Key texts: 2 Tim 3:16–17; Ps 19:7–11; Ps 119:9–11; Matt 4:4.

Why it matters: Growth in grace requires a steady diet of the Word; without it, spiritual malnutrition sets in.

4) How should Christians pray?

Answer: With reverence, sincerity, persistence, and alignment with God’s will, using the Lord’s Prayer as a model. Prayer is relational communion with God, not mechanical ritual.

Key texts: Matt 6:5–13; Phil 4:6–7; Col 4:2; 1 Thess 5:16–18.

Why it matters: Prayer shapes our desires, strengthens faith, and advances God’s kingdom purposes.

5) How do spiritual disciplines contribute to discipleship?

Answer: Disciplines like prayer, fasting, Bible reading, fellowship, confession, and service are means of grace by which God forms Christlike character in His people.

Key texts: 1 Tim 4:7–8; Acts 2:42; Matt 6:16–18; Heb 10:24–25.

Why it matters: Disciplines are not ends in themselves but channels of grace; they cultivate intimacy and obedience.

6) How do Christians handle suffering and trials?

Answer: By trusting God’s sovereignty, finding comfort in His promises, rejoicing in hope, and allowing trials to produce perseverance and maturity.

Key texts: Rom 5:1–5; Jas 1:2–4; 1 Pet 1:6–7; 2 Cor 4:16–18.

Why it matters: Suffering is not wasted; God refines faith and displays His glory through endurance.

7) What is biblical stewardship?

Answer: Managing time, talents, treasures, and creation in faithful service to God, recognizing all belongs to Him and is entrusted for His purposes.

Key texts: Gen 1:28; Ps 24:1; Matt 25:14–30; 1 Cor 4:2; 2 Cor 9:6–11.

Why it matters: Stewardship transforms work, generosity, and ecology into acts of worship.

8) How does the church community aid discipleship?

Answer: The body builds itself up in love through mutual encouragement, accountability, teaching, and shared mission. Isolation is spiritually dangerous.

Key texts: Heb 10:24–25; Acts 2:42–47; Eph 4:11–16; Rom 12:4–8.

Why it matters: Christianity is corporate as well as personal; growth happens in community.

9) What does it mean to “walk by the Spirit” in everyday life?

Answer: Yielding daily to the Spirit’s leading, rejecting the flesh’s desires, and bearing the fruit of love, joy, peace, and other Christlike virtues.

Key texts: Gal 5:16–25; Rom 8:4–6; Eph 5:18–21.

Why it matters: The Spirit-filled life is the normal Christian life; anything less quenches the Spirit’s work.

10) How do Christians live missionally in a secular culture?

Answer: By embodying the gospel in word and deed, being salt and light, defending the faith graciously, and serving with compassion while resisting compromise.

Key texts: Matt 5:13–16; 28:18–20; 1 Pet 3:15–16; Phil 2:14–16.

Why it matters: The Great Commission is for all believers; everyday life is the mission field.

Eschatology & the Blessed Hope — God’s Final Redemptive Acts

Exploring the biblical teaching on the end times, Christ’s return, final judgment, and the eternal state.

1) What does “eschatology” mean?

Answer: Eschatology is the study of “last things” — God’s final acts in history, including the return of Christ, the resurrection, judgment, and the eternal kingdom.

Key texts: Isa 65:17–25; Dan 12:2–3; 1 Cor 15; Rev 21–22.

Why it matters: Our view of the end shapes how we live in the present, fueling hope and perseverance.

2) How will Christ return?

Answer: Personally, visibly, and gloriously, to judge the living and the dead, and to consummate His kingdom. His coming will be sudden and unmistakable.

Key texts: Acts 1:9–11; Matt 24:27–31; 1 Thess 4:16–17; Rev 1:7.

Why it matters: Prevents deception by false claims and calls believers to readiness and holiness.

3) What is the “resurrection of the dead”?

Answer: At Christ’s return, all will be raised bodily — the righteous to eternal life, the wicked to judgment. This affirms the goodness of God’s creation and His intent to redeem it.

Key texts: Dan 12:2; John 5:28–29; 1 Cor 15:20–58.

Why it matters: Christianity is not escapism but bodily renewal; death is defeated, not redefined.

4) What is the final judgment?

Answer: God will judge every person according to their deeds, revealing His justice and mercy. Believers are acquitted through Christ’s righteousness; unbelievers face condemnation.

Key texts: Matt 25:31–46; Rom 14:10–12; Rev 20:11–15.

Why it matters: Affirms God’s moral order; inspires holy living and gospel urgency.

5) What is the “new heavens and new earth”?

Answer: The renewed creation where God dwells with His people forever, free from sin, death, and suffering, filled with righteousness and joy.

Key texts: Isa 65:17; 2 Pet 3:13; Rev 21–22.

Why it matters: The Christian hope is not mere survival after death but life in a perfected world with God.

6) What is the “Millennium” in Revelation 20?

Answer: A debated period in which Christ reigns. Views include premillennialism, amillennialism, and postmillennialism — differing on timing but united in affirming Christ’s ultimate victory.

Key texts: Rev 20:1–10; Isa 11; Ps 72.

Why it matters: Encourages humility in secondary issues while holding firm to the certainty of Christ’s reign.

7) What is the “Blessed Hope”?

Answer: The eager expectation of Christ’s return, when He will set all things right and bring His people into eternal joy.

Key texts: Titus 2:11–14; Phil 3:20–21; 1 Pet 1:3–9.

Why it matters: This hope fuels endurance, holiness, and joyful service now.

8) How should Christians live in light of the end times?

Answer: By being watchful, holy, and mission-focused, avoiding speculation and fear, and fixing our eyes on Christ.

Key texts: 1 Thess 5:1–11; 2 Pet 3:10–14; Matt 24:42–51.

Why it matters: Eschatology is not for curiosity alone but for shaping faithful living.

9) What about the “Antichrist” and tribulation?

Answer: Scripture warns of a final man of lawlessness and intensified opposition before Christ’s return, yet assures believers of God’s preserving power.

Key texts: 2 Thess 2:1–12; 1 John 2:18; Rev 13.

Why it matters: Prepares the church for endurance and prevents alarmism.

10) Will heaven be our eternal home?

Answer: Ultimately, God’s people will dwell in the new creation — heaven and earth united — with God Himself in unbroken fellowship forever.

Key texts: Rev 21–22; Isa 66:22–23; John 14:1–3.

Why it matters: Eternal life is not disembodied clouds but embodied joy in God’s renewed world.
