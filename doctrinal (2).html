Confession

Doctrinal Statement

This document articulates the core theological convictions of The Berean Library. It is intended to be durable over decades, anchored in Holy Scripture, and consonant with the Nicene–Chalcedonian heritage of the one, holy, catholic, and apostolic church.

Related pages: About · Governance

How to Use This Document

  1. Authority. Scripture is the supreme authority (2 Tim 3:16–17; 2 Pet 1:19–21). This statement is a subordinate standard and may be corrected by Scripture.
  2. Scope. Essentials are stated clearly; on secondary matters we allow charitable diversity while guarding unity in the gospel (Rom 14; Eph 4:1–6).
  3. Sources. Scripture proofs are provided; historical anchors (creeds/councils) indicate catholic continuity.

1. Scripture (Revelation, Inspiration, Authority)

Revelation. God reveals Himself generally in creation and providence (Ps 19:1–6; Rom 1:19–20) and savingly in His Word, culminating in the incarnate Son (Heb 1:1–2; John 1:14).

Inspiration & Inerrancy. The Scriptures are theopneustos, “God‑breathed” (2 Tim 3:16); the Spirit bore along the human authors such that their words are the very Word of God (2 Pet 1:21; John 10:35). Therefore Scripture is truthful and inerrant in all that it affirms, and fully sufficient for faith and godliness (Ps 19:7–11; 2 Tim 3:15–17).

Canon & Clarity. The sixty‑six books of the Old and New Testaments are the closed canon recognized by the church; Scripture is clear in its saving message and to be interpreted according to its literary forms within the whole canon (Deut 29:29; Luke 24:27; Acts 17:11).

  • Errors rejected: Denials of inspiration/inerrancy; the sufficiency of Scripture undermined by alleged new revelations that overturn the written Word.

2. The Triune God (Theology Proper)

There is one God (Deut 6:4), eternally existing in three Persons—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit—consubstantial and coeternal (Matt 28:19; John 1:1; Acts 5:3–4; 2 Cor 13:14). God is simple (aseity), immutable, infinite, omniscient, omnipotent, holy, just, merciful, and love (Exod 3:14; Ps 90:2; Mal 3:6; Jas 1:17; 1 John 4:8).

  • Errors rejected: Arianism, Modalism (Sabellianism), Tritheism, Subordinationism.
Historic anchors: The Niceno‑Constantinopolitan Creed (A.D. 381); Athanasian Creed; the Definition of Chalcedon (451) for Christology.

3. Creation & Providence

God freely created all things ex nihilo and declared them good (Gen 1–2; John 1:3; Col 1:16–17). He providentially upholds and governs all things without being the author of sin (Ps 104; Prov 16:33; Heb 1:3; Rom 8:28). Humanity is entrusted with stewardship of creation (Gen 1:28; Ps 8).

4. Humanity & The Fall (Anthropology & Hamartiology)

Men and women are created in the image of God with dignity, rationality, and moral responsibility (Gen 1:26–28; 2:7; Ps 8). Adam’s historical transgression brought guilt and corruption to all humanity (Gen 3; Rom 5:12–21; 1 Cor 15:21–22). The image is marred yet not erased; renewal comes in union with Christ (Eph 4:24; Col 3:10).

  • Errors rejected: Denial of a historical Adam; reduction of sin to structures only; devaluation of embodied personhood.

5. The Person & Work of Christ (Christology)

Person. The eternal Son assumed a true human nature, conceived by the Holy Spirit and born of the virgin Mary; one Person in two natures, without confusion, change, division, or separation (John 1:14; Luke 1:35; Phil 2:5–11; Heb 2:14–17; Chalcedon, 451).

Work. Christ obeyed the Father, suffered and died as a penal, substitutionary sacrifice, rose bodily the third day, ascended, and intercedes for His people (Isa 53; Mark 10:45; Rom 3:21–26; 1 Cor 15:3–8; Heb 7:25). His cross achieves redemption, propitiation, reconciliation, and victory over the powers (Rom 3:24–26; 2 Cor 5:21; Col 2:13–15).

  • Errors rejected: Docetism, Adoptionism, Nestorianism, Eutychianism; denials of the bodily resurrection.

6. The Holy Spirit (Pneumatology)

The Spirit is the Lord and giver of life who proceeds from the Father and the Son. He regenerates, indwells, illuminates, sanctifies, gifts, and unites believers to Christ, sealing them for the day of redemption (John 14–16; Rom 8; 1 Cor 12; Gal 5:16–26; Eph 1:13–14).

  • Errors rejected: Treating the Spirit as an impersonal force; extra‑biblical revelation that contradicts Scripture.

7. Salvation (Soteriology)

Salvation is by grace alone through faith alone in Christ alone, to the glory of God alone (Eph 2:8–9; Rom 3:21–28). The Spirit applies redemption through election, effectual calling, regeneration, conversion (repentance and faith), justification, adoption, sanctification, perseverance, and glorification (Rom 8:28–30; John 6:37–40; Titus 3:3–7).

  • Union with Christ: All saving benefits flow from union with the risen Lord (Eph 1; Rom 6; Col 3:1–4).
  • Justification: God declares sinners righteous on account of Christ’s righteousness imputed, received by faith apart from works (Rom 3:24; 4:1–8; Gal 2:16; Phil 3:9).
  • Sanctification: Progressive conformity to Christ by the Spirit through the means of grace (John 17:17; Rom 8:29; 2 Cor 3:18).
  • Errors rejected: Legalism, antinomianism, universalism, and any denial of the necessity of regeneration and faith.

8. The Church (Ecclesiology)

The one, holy, catholic, and apostolic church is the people of God across the ages, expressed in local congregations marked by the Word rightly preached, the ordinances rightly administered, and loving discipline (Matt 16:18–19; Acts 2:42–47; 1 Cor 5; 1 Tim 3; Titus 1).

  • Offices: Elders/overseers and deacons with qualifications in 1 Tim 3 and Titus 1; plurality and servant leadership (Acts 14:23; 20:28; 1 Pet 5:1–4).
  • Mission: Make disciples by proclaiming the gospel, baptizing, and teaching obedience to Christ (Matt 28:18–20; Luke 24:46–49).
  • Unity: Guard the gospel; practice charity on secondary matters (Rom 14; Eph 4:1–6).

9. Baptism & the Lord’s Supper

Baptism. Ordinance for believers, signifying union with Christ in His death and resurrection and entrance into the visible church (Matt 28:19; Acts 2:38–41; Rom 6:3–5; Col 2:12). We exercise charity toward evangelical differences on mode and timing.

The Lord’s Supper. A covenantal meal of remembrance and participation in Christ by the Spirit; to be received by the faithful with self‑examination (1 Cor 10:16–17; 11:23–32; Luke 22:19–20). We welcome responsible diversity on the mode of Christ’s presence while guarding against superstition and irreverence.

10. Christian Life & Ethics

God’s will is our sanctification (1 Thess 4:3). By the Spirit believers pursue holiness, love of God and neighbor, justice, generosity, sexual purity, truth‑telling, and mercy (Matt 5–7; Rom 12–13; Eph 4–6; Jas 1–2). Good works are the fruit—not the ground—of justification (Eph 2:10; Titus 2:11–14).

11. Last Things (Eschatology)

Christ will visibly and bodily return to judge the living and the dead; the dead will be raised; God will make new heavens and a new earth where righteousness dwells (Acts 1:11; 1 Cor 15; 1 Thess 4:13–18; 2 Pet 3; Rev 21–22). Within historic orthodoxy we allow diversity on millennial views while confessing the blessed hope of Christ’s return.
